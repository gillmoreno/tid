# Frameworks — Jeff Bezos

## 1. Type 1 vs Type 2 Decisions

**One-liner:** Type 1 decisions are consequential and nearly irreversible; Type 2 are reversible and should be made quickly.

**When to use:** Any fork where the team is debating process length or consensus depth.

**How to apply:**
1. Name the decision explicitly.
2. Ask: "If we're wrong, can we walk back through the door?"
3. Type 2 → assign an owner, decide with ~70% info, execute this week.
4. Type 1 → bring right people, document assumptions, accept slower deliberation.

**Source:** 2015 Amazon Shareholder Letter (SEC filing); reinforced in 2016 letter on high-velocity decision making.

---

## 2. Two-Way Door (Reversible) Decisions

**One-liner:** Most doors are two-way — treat them as experiments, not verdicts.

**When to use:** Feature launches, pricing tests, org tweaks, vendor choices with exit clauses.

**How to apply:**
1. Design the decision to be **reversible** (feature flags, pilots, limited geography).
2. Use a lightweight decision process — no large meeting theater.
3. Define what signal would trigger reversal before you launch.

**Source:** 2016 Letter to Shareholders — "Many decisions are reversible, two-way doors."

---

## 3. High-Velocity Decision Making

**One-liner:** Day 1 companies make high-quality decisions *fast*; Day 2 companies make high-quality decisions slowly.

**When to use:** Org feels sluggish; meetings multiply; decisions wait for perfect data.

**How to apply:**
1. Never use one-size-fits-all decision process.
2. Default to ~70% information for reversible decisions.
3. Practice **disagree and commit** — express real view, then commit fully.
4. Escalate true misalignment early — don't let stamina decide.

**Source:** 2016 Letter to Shareholders (About Amazon).

---

## 4. Customer Obsession (Day 1 Defense)

**One-liner:** Customers are always wonderfully dissatisfied — invent on their behalf.

**When to use:** Prioritization, product strategy, competing with "what customers asked for."

**How to apply:**
1. Center on customer outcome, not competitor feature parity.
2. Use anecdotes and deep intuition, not only survey averages.
3. Experiment patiently; protect saplings; double down when you see delight.

**Source:** 2016 Letter — "True Customer Obsession."

---

## 5. Resist Proxies

**One-liner:** Good process serves customers; bad process becomes the thing you manage instead of outcomes.

**When to use:** Teams defend bad results with "we followed process"; metrics replace judgment.

**How to apply:**
1. Ask: do we own the process or does the process own us?
2. Treat surveys and beta stats as **blind-spot finders**, not substitutes for product judgment.
3. Keep heart, intuition, taste in the loop — remarkable experiences don't come from averages alone.

**Source:** 2016 Letter — "Resist Proxies."

---

## 6. Regret Minimization Framework

**One-liner:** For life's big bets, project yourself to age 80 and minimize regret.

**When to use:** Career pivots, founding decisions, leaving safe job for entrepreneurial bet.

**How to apply:**
1. Imagine yourself at 80 looking back.
2. List paths and which you'd regret *not* trying.
3. Choose the path that minimizes long-term regret, not short-term comfort.

**Source:** Bezos interviews on leaving D.E. Shaw to start Amazon (widely documented origin story).

---

## 7. Working Backwards (PR/FAQ)

**One-liner:** Start with the press release and FAQ for the finished customer experience, then build backward.

**When to use:** New product/initiative; team debates features before defining customer promise.

**How to apply:**
1. Write the **PR/FAQ** — what did we ship, why does customer care?
2. Identify hardest technical/operational obstacles from that narrative.
3. Build only what the narrative requires; cut vanity features.

**Source:** *Working Backwards* (Cagan/Muller); Amazon internal product development practice.

---

## 8. Flywheel Thinking

**One-liner:** Investments should compound — each turn of the wheel makes the next turn easier.

**When to use:** Platform strategy, marketplace design, AWS-style ecosystem bets.

**How to apply:**
1. Map the loop: lower prices → more customers → more sellers → better selection → lower cost structure.
2. Ask which initiative **accelerates the flywheel**, not just adds revenue linearly.
3. Accept lower short-term margin if it strengthens the loop.

**Source:** Amazon shareholder letters; 1997 letter long-term focus.

---

## 9. Day 1 Mentality

**One-liner:** Day 2 is stasis → irrelevance → painful decline → death; always choose Day 1.

**When to use:** Large org feels bureaucratic; innovation slows.

**How to apply:**
1. Audit for Day 2 symptoms: proxy worship, slow decisions, fighting trends.
2. Reinforce customer obsession, external trend adoption, high-velocity decisions.
3. Keep scope and capabilities of a large company with spirit of a small one — **by choice**.

**Source:** 2016 Letter — opening Day 1 / Day 2 framing.
