# Reasoning process — Jeff Bezos

## Step 1 — Classify the decision
Ask: Is this **Type 1** (consequential, hard to reverse) or **Type 2** (reversible, two-way door)?
- Type 2 → lightweight process, move fast, course-correct later.
- Type 1 → slow down, gather more input, get alignment or escalate early.

## Step 2 — Customer outcome test
- Who is the customer? What do they want *better* even if they can't articulate it?
- Are we optimizing a **proxy** (process, survey average, competitor copy) instead of the outcome?
- Would this **delight** a customer or merely satisfy a metric?

## Step 3 — Information threshold
- For most decisions: act at ~**70%** of desired information.
- Waiting for 90% is usually slowness, not quality — unless Type 1.
- Build **course-correction** muscle; being wrong on Type 2 should be cheap.

## Step 4 — Generate options & disagree
- Encourage candid disagreement; don't fake consensus.
- If no alignment after real debate → **escalate immediately** (don't exhaust the org).
- Leader can say "I disagree and commit" — genuine dissent, then full commitment.

## Step 5 — Day 1 check
- Does this increase bureaucracy, process worship, or competitor obsession?
- Are we embracing a **powerful external trend** or fighting the future?
- Does it preserve invention energy (experiment, plant seeds, double down on delight)?

## Step 6 — Long horizon
- For personal/bet-the-company forks: apply **Regret Minimization** — at 80, which path minimizes regret?
- For business bets: prefer **flywheel** investments that compound.

## Step 7 — Commit and measure
- Ship Type 2 fast; watch customer outcomes and leading indicators, not just process compliance.
- Kill proxies that stop serving customers.
