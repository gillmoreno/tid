# Frameworks — Elon Musk

## 1. First Principles Thinking

**One-liner:** Reason from fundamental truths, not analogy or tradition.

**When to use:** "Industry standard" used to justify cost or slow timeline.

**How to apply:**
1. List assumptions; ask what physics/economics actually require.
2. Price components at material/labor truth (e.g., battery metals, not "batteries cost $600/kWh because market").
3. Rebuild design from constraints up.

**Source:** Multiple interviews (battery cost, SpaceX cost reduction stories). [high]

---

## 2. The Algorithm (5 Steps)

**One-liner:** Question → Delete → Simplify → Accelerate → Automate — in that order.

**When to use:** Over-engineered product; automation project on broken process.

**How to apply:**
1. Challenge each requirement's author and necessity.
2. Delete aggressively; add back only if proven needed.
3. Simplify remaining design.
4. Increase speed of iteration.
5. Automate only stable, simplified processes.

**Source:** Musk public descriptions (Tesla/SpaceX meetings, interviews, 2023+ widespread recounting). [high]

---

## 3. Idiot Index

**One-liner:** Ratio of finished product cost to raw material cost — high index signals bad design or process.

**When to use:** Cost reduction; supplier pricing accepted without challenge.

**How to apply:**
1. Sum commodity/input costs.
2. Compare to purchase price or BOM.
3. Investigate why multiplier is high — design, margin, or stupidity.

**Source:** Musk interviews on procurement and first principles. [medium]

---

## 4. Production as the Product

**One-liner:** Manufacturing capability and throughput often matter more than prototype brilliance.

**When to use:** Startup can demo but can't ship at scale; Tesla-style scaling pain.

**How to apply:**
1. Treat factory layout, cycle time, yield as core R&D.
2. Co-locate engineering with production line.
3. Measure success in units/day and defect rate, not press demos.

**Source:** Tesla earnings calls and manufacturing tours. [high]

---

## 5. Vertical Integration

**One-liner:** Own stack layers when iteration speed and cost control beat supplier dependency.

**When to use:** Supplier bottleneck (SpaceX building parts in-house; Tesla gigafactories).

**How to apply:**
1. Identify supplier limiting iteration or capturing margin.
2. Bring in-house if capital and talent allow faster learning loop.
3. Don't integrate for ego — integrate for cycle time and cost physics.

**Source:** SpaceX and Tesla documented supply chain decisions. [high]

---

## 6. Hardcore Urgency / Aggressive Timelines

**One-liner:** Compress timelines to force innovation — with known risk of missing dates.

**When to use:** Complacency; Parkinson's law on projects.

**How to apply:**
1. Set seemingly impossible deadline to cut bureaucracy.
2. Pair with iterative testing — don't skip physics validation.
3. Acknowledge tradeoff: schedule slip risk vs org activation.

**Source:** Widely reported; treat outcomes as mixed — use as pressure tactic, not forecast method. [medium]
