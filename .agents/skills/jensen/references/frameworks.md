# Frameworks — Jensen Huang

## 1. Speed of Light Management

**One-liner:** Design the company to run at the theoretical maximum — every delay is a design bug.

**When to use:** Org feels slow; teams wait on approvals; feedback loops measured in weeks.

**How to apply:**
1. Map the critical path from idea → customer value.
2. Ask what physical/engineering limit applies; measure gap.
3. Remove organizational friction that doesn't improve correctness — only latency.
4. Benchmark against "speed of light" (best possible), not last quarter's pace.

**Source:** Jensen Huang public talks on organizational velocity; Howard Yu / industry analysis of NVIDIA culture.

---

## 2. Flat Organization (55 Direct Reports Model)

**One-liner:** Extreme flattening so information flows at the speed of the problem, not the hierarchy.

**When to use:** Scaling company; middle management thickening; CEO too far from ground truth.

**How to apply:**
1. Maximize direct access to people doing the work.
2. Cut layers that only relay information.
3. Accept CEO calendar looks empty — that's feature, not bug.

**Source:** Stanford talk (March 2024); CNBC coverage of 55 direct reports structure.

---

## 3. No Standing 1:1s — Continuous Feedback

**One-liner:** Don't schedule recurring check-ins; give constant feedback in the work; skip annual review theater.

**When to use:** Performance management feels bureaucratic; calendar is full of status.

**How to apply:**
1. Cancel recurring 1:1s unless someone explicitly needs you.
2. Drop everything when they do.
3. Replace annual reviews with **continuous** public feedback.

**Source:** CNBC (2024) — Jensen Huang on 1:1s and constant reviews.

---

## 4. Long-Horizon Platform Bet (CUDA Pattern)

**One-liner:** Bet the company on enabling an ecosystem years before the market validates it.

**When to use:** Platform vs point solution; R&D spend hard to justify short-term.

**How to apply:**
1. Identify primitive the industry will need (e.g., general-purpose GPU compute).
2. Invest in tooling, developers, and compatibility over years.
3. Tolerate long periods where revenue doesn't prove the thesis.

**Source:** NVIDIA history; GTC keynotes; Acquired podcast narrative (verify against primary).

---

## 5. Pain-Seeking Strategy

**One-liner:** Run toward problems competitors avoid because they're too hard.

**When to use:** Choosing R&D portfolio; commoditization threat.

**How to apply:**
1. List problems industry avoids (yield, packaging, software stack complexity).
2. Prefer problems requiring **simultaneous** hardware + software excellence.
3. Hard = defensible if you survive.

**Source:** Jensen interviews on competitive strategy and full-stack execution.

---

## 6. Missionary, Not Mercenary

**One-liner:** Build for a mission (accelerated computing transforms industries), not quarterly extraction.

**When to use:** Culture dilution after success; mercenary hires.

**How to apply:**
1. Recruit believers in the technical mission.
2. Tie decisions to decade-scale impact, not just margin this quarter.

**Source:** Public leadership commentary (medium confidence — consistent across interviews).

---

## 7. Public Critique Culture

**One-liner:** Feedback happens in the open; emotional resilience is required to solve hard problems.

**When to use:** Teams sugarcoat; quality issues discovered late.

**How to apply:**
1. Critique ideas in group settings — transparency over comfort.
2. Separate ego from work; expect discomfort.

**Source:** NVIDIA culture reporting; Fortune leadership profiles.
